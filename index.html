How Did the Pandemic Reshape Boston's Bike-Share?

An analysis of Bluebikes ridership across 2019, 2022, and 2023

DS4200 - Information Presentation and Data Visualization

Introduction

Bluebikes is Boston's public bike-share system and at this point it covers most of the city. When COVID hit, public transit usage dropped and transportation in Boston changed significantly. We wanted to dig into the data to understand what happened to bike-sharing through the pandemic. Did it recover? Did it change? And if so, how?

We selected three years of Bluebikes trip data to compare: 2019 which is the last full year before the pandemic, 2022 when things were starting to recover, and 2023 which gives us a more recent picture. The goal here is to illuminate patterns that could help Bluebikes, as a company, make better decisions about where to locate stations, when to move bikes around, and how to think about their changing rider base.

Our analysis addresses five questions:

  1. How has overall ridership changed and is the mix of Subscribers vs Casual riders shifting?
  2. Have the peak riding times changed since people started working from home?
  3. How has the station network grown geographically?
  4. How much has the Casual rider segment grown in absolute terms, not just percentage?
  5. Are people riding differently in terms of how long their trips are?

About the Data

Our data comes from the Bluebikes System Data page where they publish trip history files every quarter. Each row in the raw data is a single trip: one person on one bike going from one station to another.

We downloaded all 36 monthly CSV files across the three years which came out to about 9.97 million trips total. After cleaning out bad data like trips shorter than a minute or longer than 24 hours and rows with missing coordinates, we ended up with about 9.89 million usable trips. We then aggregated that full dataset into focused summary tables, one per visualization. That means every count and average you see in the charts below is directly from the complete data, not a sample.

The main features we worked with include start and stop times, hour of day, day of week, station names and coordinates, trip duration, and user type which is either Subscriber or Casual.

1. Monthly Ridership Over Time

Click a year in the legend to isolate it. Hover any point for the Subscriber and Casual breakdown.

Takeaway

At first glance, it's clear to see that 2022 and 2023 both exceed 2019 in total ridership across every month. The system did not just recover from the pandemic, it grew. The seasonal pattern is consistently observed, with summer peaks and winter dips, but even in the winter months 2023 saw nearly double the ridership of the same months in 2019. That suggests a broader and more committed rider base has formed. If you hover over the points you can also see that Casual riders are making up an increasing share of the total, especially in the warmer months.

One thing worth flagging is the unusual spike in September 2022 which sits noticeably above the surrounding months. We are not sure if this reflects an actual usage surge or a reporting artifact from Bluebikes but it stands out from the broader trend and would be worth investigating further if we had access to more context on the data collection process.

2. Activity Heatmap: When Do People Ride?

All three years are shown with a shared color scale so you can compare directly. Hover over any cell for exact trip counts.

Takeaway

In 2019 the commute pattern is very clear. You can see the dark bands at 8am and 5 to 6pm on weekdays which is exactly what you would expect from a system dominated by commuters. But when you look at 2022 and 2023 those peaks aren't quite as sharp, instead, usage is more evenly spread out through the day. This likely can be attributed to the growing trend in hybrid and remote work. Weekends also look busier relative to weekdays than they did in the past. This is important for bike rebalancing. Rather than simply moving bikes around twice a day during rush hour, Bluebikes may need to consider moving bikes more continuously throughout the day.

3. Station Map: Where Do People Ride?

Original (2019) Added by 2022 Added in 2023

Click the year buttons to switch between years. Scroll to zoom in or out, drag to pan around. Hover over any station to see its name, total trips, departures, and arrivals. Circle size reflects total trip volume and color indicates when the station was first active.

Takeaway

The network has grown noticeably across the three years, from around 355 locations in 2019 to 448 in 2022 and 459 in 2023. Coloring stations by when they first appeared makes the shape of this growth clearer. The orange dots are the original 2019 footprint, the blue dots are the roughly 124 new stations added by 2022, and the green dots are the handful of additional stations that opened in 2023. Most of the expansion actually happened between 2019 and 2022. In 2023 the system mostly consolidated rather than expanded. You can also see that the new stations tend to sit on the outer edges, filling in neighborhoods like Chelsea, Newton, and parts of Brookline that were not served before. The busiest stations, shown as the largest circles, are still concentrated downtown and around the university corridor. For Bluebikes this points to two things. The rapid 2019 to 2022 push was an aggressive expansion that slowed down afterward, and the newer outer stations would benefit from promotion to build up ridership since they currently show smaller circles than the core.

4. Casual Rider Growth: How Much Has the Segment Actually Expanded?

Hover over any bar to see the exact annual trip count for that rider type.

Takeaway

Viz 1 showed the Casual rider share going up in percentage terms, but that could have happened just because Subscribers were shrinking. This chart confirms it is not just a relative shift. Casual trips roughly doubled from around 440 thousand in 2019 to nearly 940 thousand in both 2022 and 2023. Subscribers also grew but at a slower rate. So the Casual segment is genuinely expanding in raw numbers, not just in proportion, which strengthens the case for Bluebikes to invest in products tailored to casual use like day passes and weekend promotions.

5. Trip Duration: How Has Riding Behavior Changed?

The chart is split into two panels. The top chart is an overview of the full duration range. Drag across a section of it to select a range. The bottom chart is a zoomed detail view that shows the bars grouped side by side for the selected range. Use the Rider type filter to compare Subscribers vs Casual riders.

Takeaway

At first glance, trip durations look similar across all three years with most rides peaking around 5 to 10 minutes. But the interesting part comes when you filter to Casual only. Casual riders take significantly longer trips, around 19 minutes on average compared to about 10 for Subscribers. This can be explained as Subscribers are mostly commuters doing the same short route every day, while Casual riders are more likely exploring or running errands. Combined with the growth we saw in the previous chart, Bluebikes should consider how their pricing and dock availability can be adjusted for these longer and less predictable trip patterns.

Summary and Conclusions

After looking at the data across all three years, a few things stood out to us.

1. Casual riders are becoming a much bigger part of the picture

The share of Casual rides has grown significantly since 2019 and so has the raw count, roughly doubling between 2019 and 2022. This is not just a summer tourism story anymore, the increase carries throughout the year. We think Bluebikes should adjust their strategy by investing in Casual friendly options, such as improved day passes, tourist oriented routes, and weekend promotions.

2. People are not just riding during rush hour anymore

The classic commute peaks at 8am and 5pm have flattened and usage is more evenly spread throughout the day. This can likely be attributed to the rise in remote and hybrid work. For Bluebikes that means the old model, rebalancing bikes twice a day during rush hour, will need to shift toward something more continuous. The increase in weekend ridership also warrants some attention to ensure there are enough bikes for Casual riders.

3. The station network grew fast and then leveled off

Between 2019 and 2022 Bluebikes added around 124 new stations, growing the network from roughly 355 to 448 locations. After that the expansion slowed down and only about 27 more stations were added in 2023. The newer stations sit mostly on the outer edges and show lower usage than the established core, as they are still building up ridership. The demand is there, Bluebikes should continue growing the network and focus on targeting any gaps in coverage.

Future Work

There are a few directions we would like to explore in future analysis. Adding weather data would help disentangle seasonal effects from pandemic effects. Looking at station to station flow patterns could help optimize rebalancing routes. And if Bluebikes begins reporting demographic data again, we'd like to look into whether or not the rider base is getting more diverse over time.
